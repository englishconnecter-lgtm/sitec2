Jo the First is presented as a dictator who rules through fear. When he wants something, people hurry to him, and anyone who complains risks being banished. From the outside, his rule appears brutal and unjust.

JoJo, however, grows up inside the castle and sees a very different version of reality. He does not witness the fear or suffering of ordinary people; instead, he sees his father’s power, the servants’ , and constant praise. As a result, his moral outlook is shaped by an extremely upbringing.

When JoJo inherits his father’s role, his actions are clearly wrong, but the case raises a deeper question: does he bear full moral for them? The example helps to less extreme cases where people’s values have been formed by their environment.

According to the Deep Self View, people are responsible when their actions reflect their deeper values, unless they are acting under or another external force. Susan Wolf challenges this by asking how those values were formed in the first place. In JoJo’s case, his “deep self” may have been by the moral world he inherited.

The debate becomes even more complex when JoJo’s sister, JoJa, is introduced. 7. Grammar focus: concession in academic argument

One focused grammar item: use concession structures to acknowledge an opposing idea before developing your own argument. This is especially useful for C1 discussion of moral responsibility.

Grammar point

Concession means admitting that another point may be true, while showing that it does not completely defeat your argument.

Useful structures:

  • Although / Even though + clause, ...
    Although JoJo’s upbringing shaped his values, this does not automatically remove all responsibility.
  • While it is true that + clause, ...
    While it is true that JoJo was raised in an abnormal environment, he may still have had some capacity for moral reflection.
  • Despite / In spite of + noun or -ing, ...
    Despite being raised by a tyrant, JoJo might still be judged for continuing the same cruelty.
  • Admittedly, ... However, ...
    Admittedly, JoJo’s moral education was deeply distorted. However, this does not prove that he lacked all agency.
  • Even if + clause, ...
    Even if his values were shaped by his father, society may still need to hold him accountable.

Practice A: choose the best concession structure

Answers: 1 Although, 2 Despite, 3 While, 4 Given that, 5 Even though.

Practice B: complete the argument

Example answers:
1. Although JoJo’s moral world was shaped by his father, he may still be accountable for continuing a system of cruelty.
2. While it is true that upbringing influences character, it does not completely erase the possibility of moral reflection.
3. Despite being exposed only to tyranny, JoJo might have noticed the suffering caused by his rule.
4. Admittedly, JoJo’s case makes blame more complicated. However, explanation is not the same as excuse.
5. Even if determinism weakens the idea of free choice, society may still need responsibility for practical and ethical reasons.

Practice C: rewrite using concession

Possible rewrites:
1. Although JoJo had an abnormal upbringing, he still harmed others.
2. While it is true that his father shaped his values, JoJo may still be morally responsible.
3. Despite being influenced by his environment, JoJo cannot be fully excused.
4. Admittedly, determinism is persuasive. However, it creates problems for punishment.

Five discussion questions

  1. To what extent do you think a person’s upbringing determines their moral character? Can people ever truly overcome their early conditioning?
  2. The video presents the Deep Self View and Susan Wolf’s critique of it. Which position do you find more convincing, and why?
  3. How should the legal system account for a criminal’s upbringing when determining their sentence? Should a deprived childhood reduce punishment?
  4. What does JoJa’s existence as a counterexample imply about JoJo’s moral responsibility? Is it a strong argument?
  5. If no one is truly responsible for who they become, what are the implications for guilt, punishment and forgiveness in society?

Model opening: "Most legal systems already recognise mitigating circumstances, and I would argue this is both philosophically sound and practically necessary. A childhood characterised by abuse, deprivation, or the absence of any moral education clearly affects the conditions under which a person develops — and justice that ignores these conditions risks being merely punitive rather than genuinely fair."
Useful language: by extension… this undermines the claim that… it does not necessarily follow that… one could posit that… the implication being… the counterexample has limited force because…
Model opening: "JoJa's existence is philosophically significant because it challenges the assumption that JoJo's tyranny was an inevitable outcome of his environment. If someone raised in identical conditions chose differently, it suggests that JoJo retained some capacity for moral choice. However, this argument is not without problems — individual variation in temperament or circumstance might explain the divergence without restoring full moral agency to JoJo."
Useful language: the ramifications of… were this to be the case… it would stand to reason that… society would be compelled to… a fundamentally different conception of…
Model opening: "Were we to accept a thoroughgoing incompatibilism — that no one is truly responsible for who they become — the implications would be profound. The retributive model of justice, which relies on the concept of deserved punishment, would lose its moral foundation entirely. In its place, we would be forced to adopt a purely consequentialist framework: punishment only justifiable insofar as it deters, rehabilitates, or protects society — not as something a person deserves."
