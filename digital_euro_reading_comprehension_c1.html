Article extract

Read the text carefully. Pay attention to cause and effect, contrast, and the policy concerns raised in the final paragraphs.

Money is changing at a pace that would have seemed unthinkable even a few years ago. In just the last ten years, credit cards, debit cards and mobile phones have displaced cash, with notes and coins being used less and less as physical money gives way to digital.

The European Central Bank (ECB) is working towards the launch of the digital euro: an electronic version of public money designed to provide a safe, secure public option for digital payments. More than 130 countries are exploring or have already launched their own digital currency.

The digital euro would be stored in a virtual wallet and would not generate interest. It would function like cash: a balance that does not depend on the solvency of any private financial institution. Crucially, it would also work without an internet connection, enabling payments in rural areas or during network outages.

Supporters argue that the project could strengthen EU monetary sovereignty by ensuring that citizens still have access to public money in an increasingly digital economy. A possible official launch has been discussed for 2029, provided test phases are satisfactory and financial stability is not threatened.

One of the biggest concerns is privacy. The ECB has proposed that neither governments nor central banks should be able to see citizens’ everyday transactions. The digital euro would also include a holding cap of around €3,000 per person to reduce the risk that people move too much money away from commercial banks.

Critics warn that a digital currency could create new forms of exclusion. People without reliable internet access, modern devices or strong digital skills may find the system difficult to use. There are also cybersecurity concerns: a major breach affecting millions of users could seriously damage trust in the currency.

The debate is therefore not only technical. It raises wider questions about trust, financial stability, privacy, digital inclusion and the future role of cash in democratic societies.
