4. Five-paragraph transcript gap-fill

Choose the correct word or phrase from each dropdown. The five paragraphs are adapted from the transcript for classroom use.

Choking under pressure means failing to perform well at the crucial moment, even when you have the necessary ability. The video explains that this often happens when pressure interferes with and makes the performer focus on fears rather than on the task itself.

One explanation is that stressful thoughts compete for limited mental resources. When people become with worries about failure, judgement or consequences, there is less working memory available for what they actually need to do.

Another explanation is that experts can start to overthink skills that normally happen automatically. A tennis player, musician or speaker may become too focused on the of performance, which can interrupt smooth, well-practised actions.

The video suggests that people can prepare by practising under conditions that feel similar to real pressure. This helps them become more to stress, so the final performance feels less unusual or threatening.

A useful strategy is to develop a short routine and keep attention on the . Instead of analysing every movement, the performer focuses on the outcome they want to achieve.

8. Discussion

Use the useful language table first, then discuss the five questions below. Aim to give examples, counterarguments and practical advice.

FunctionUseful language
Introducing nuanceIt would be too simplistic to claim that pressure is always harmful because...
Balancing two ideasWhile pressure can sharpen attention, it can also overload working memory.
Challenging an assumptionThis assumes that all performers respond to stress in the same way, which may not be true.
Giving evidenceThe transcript suggests that performance depends not only on skill, but also on attention.
ConcludingOverall, the most effective strategy seems to be combining preparation, pressure practice and external focus.

Five discussion questions

  1. Is choking mainly a psychological problem, a preparation problem, or a problem of attention?
  2. Should schools, workplaces and sports teams deliberately train people under pressure?
  3. Can routines become a useful support, or can they turn into superstition?
  4. How could the video’s advice apply to presentations, interviews or exams?
  5. Which strategy is most useful for avoiding choking: pressure practice, a routine, external focus, or changing how we think about failure?
